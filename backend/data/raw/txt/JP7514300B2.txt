Optical coherence tomography (OCT) is a technique for obtaining high-resolution cross-sectional images of tissues and materials, allowing real-time visualization. The goal of OCT techniques is to measure the time delay of light using interference optics and techniques such as Fourier transform and Michelson interferometers. Light from a light source is split by a splitter (e.g., a beam splitter) and sent to a reference arm and a sample (or measurement) arm. The reference beam is reflected from a reference mirror (partially reflective or other reflective element) in the reference arm, and the sample beam is reflected or scattered from the sample in the sample arm. Both beams are combined (or recombined) at the splitter, producing interference fringes. The output of the interferometer is detected by one or more detectors, such as photodiodes or multi-array cameras, in one or more devices, such as a spectrometer (e.g., a Fourier transform infrared spectrometer). Interference fringes are produced when the path length of the sample arm matches the path length of the reference arm within the coherence length of the light source. By evaluating the output beams, the spectrum of the input radiation can be derived as a function of frequency. The frequency of the interference fringes corresponds to the distance between the sample and reference arms. The higher the frequency, the greater the difference in path length. Single mode fiber can be used for OCT optical probes, and double clad fiber can be used for fluorescence and/or spectroscopy.

Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) has been dramatically improved by innovative imaging modalities such as coronary angiography and intravascular imaging. Coronary angiography provides longitudinal silhouettes of the coronary arteries, while intravascular imaging modalities provide cross-sectional information of the coronary arteries. As intravascular imaging modalities such as intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) and optical coherence tomography (OCT) provide more precise information about vascular lesions (e.g., lumen size, plaque morphology, implanted devices), systems have been developed that allow physicians to link (i.e., coregist) ex vivo and in vivo imaging modalities. One currently available method requires the generation of vascular centerlines for coregistration from angiographic data acquired simultaneously during IVUS/OCT pullback. Another method requires the generation of imaging catheter pathways from angiographic data acquired prior to IVUS/OCT pullback via user input.

A system that allows a physician to combine two different imaging modalities (e.g., including both coronary angiography and intravascular imaging) involves co-registration.
